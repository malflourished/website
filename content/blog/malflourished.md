---

Here I am looking at my completed website — the version that's taken me over 16 years to make. I'm clicking through the pages, revisiting the long list of projects I've worked on over the years, and going over the little details I would have never thought to add on my own. I can finally see that vision of my work living in a space online I can call my own.

And I'm unfulfilled. It doesn't feel like a collaboration, although I think it has been.

---

Well, that's not entirely true — the sixteen years thing, I mean. I've had several Wix portfolio sites that were built and then never again updated. A few half-attempted Squarespace template sites to sell art prints. More recent attempts through Framer, which kept selling me the dream of finally creating that engaging website I always wanted. I'm sure all these half-baked sites now live somewhere in the web archives of expired URLs and broken domains. But the ideas were always there, just not a strong vision, exactly.

And it wouldn't have happened without AI. This site exists now. I'm happy with it. It *exists*.

So why do I feel indifferent toward it?

---

I've always been a dreamer, which I'd differentiate from being an ideas guy. An ideas guy can generally carry some level of follow-through, even if the attempt is minimal. My dreams rarely survive to execution. Ideas are a dime a dozen — it's a rare thing to take one and actually put it out into the world in a finished form.

AI is changing that. Over the past six months I've built After Effects plugins, workflows, apps, widgets, games — things I would have never thought possible a few years ago. I had some ideas I've built some things. They're out in the world. How come I feel nothing toward those achievements?

Maybe it's because everything I've created recently was built entirely through the lens of AI — through what can be made purely in natural language, by talking to an LLM. I sit down, ask for a tool, and have a working prototype in a few minutes. The magic is there. But it's overshadowed by the fact that I just sat there *watching* it being built.

The code populates in the terminal window as the LLM thinks through challenges that my brain has no point of reference for, yet somehow structures the output into something I imagined. I'm told repeatedly that is a tool, but at what point does a tool stop being a tool and become something more? Would a hammer stop being a tool if I could ask it to hit the nail and it does? The hammer becomes something else. It becomes a worker.

That feeling is what I've started calling *malflourished*.

---

It's taken me a few days to get this far in writing this post — something an LLM would generate in a matter of seconds. But at least I can say these thoughts are my own, right? I sat at my computer and typed the words out myself. But how would anyone know? These words were typed, not written.

Anything that can be typed now carries the weight of being influenced by the pull of an agent. Is this post even mine? Are the insights within it mine? I think so. But the question doesn't go away.

There's a monkey's paw element to creativity now. It's as if humanity made a collective wish where all ideas can be manifested — but there won't be any memory of actually creating them. Ideas go out into the world with minimal friction, and we'll have no real memories tied to the act of making. When you remove the process, and it gets distilled down to idea → finished product, something changes in the psyche. Maybe it's the friction itself that was part of the art. Maybe it's the humanity — the anchor that makes it relatable. My thoughts on this are constantly evolving.

I feel it in everything I build now. Is what I create my own, or do I have a co-creator in everything I make? Am I co-creator? An assistant? 

---

What I do know is that it's never been easier to create. And it's never been harder to feel a sense of accomplishment.

The barriers to making things are crumbling. The feeling of having overcome something — the breakthroughs, the hardship, the earned satisfaction — has never felt further away. Making things and putting them out into the world is now easier than ever, but it’s unclear if creatives are the ones benefiting. Anyways, I hope you enjoy the website. Someone should.
