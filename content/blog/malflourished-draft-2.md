Sixteen years is a long time to want something you never made. But I've been thinking about it wrong — the sixteen years weren't a delay. They *were* the thing. The longing, the failed Wix sites, the Squarespace subscriptions I canceled after two weeks, the Framer experiments that died in draft — that was the making. It just didn't look like making. It looked like failure.

Now the site exists. I launched it in a week. And it's good — genuinely better than anything I would have designed alone. I'm looking at it right now and I feel the way you feel when someone else eats your food. Satisfied, technically. Not fed.

This is what being malflourished means.

The word came to me watching code populate a terminal window — line after line of something I didn't write and couldn't have written, assembling itself into exactly what I'd asked for. Somewhere in that gap between my words and its output, something dissolved. Not the product. The product is fine. Something in *me* dissolved. Some layer of identity that had been quietly built from the friction of struggling to make things.

I've been thinking about what that friction actually was. Not the inefficiency. Not the wasted time. The friction was *proof*. It was evidence that the thing cost me something — that I was the right size for the difficulty of making it. When you struggle to build something, the struggle is a mirror. It reflects back: *this is what you're capable of. This is where your edges are. Now push against them.* AI removes the mirror. You never find out where your edges are because you never reach them. What you lose isn't just the memory of making. You lose the calibration.

There's a specific feeling I've been trying to name — the feeling of watching something be built *for you* in real time. It's not quite helplessness. Not awe, exactly. It's closer to watching someone else carry your boxes. Relief and shame, braided. You know you could have done it. You chose not to. And now the boxes are inside and you're not sure if you live there.

I've built more in the last six months than in the prior ten years. After Effects plugins. Games. Tools. Workflows. The output is real. And I look at all of it with this strange proprietorial distance — like a developer who bought a house he didn't build. The deed is mine. The walls are mine. But I didn't pour the foundation, and I'd have no idea where to start if something needed fixing.

Maybe that's fine. Maybe that's just the new arrangement and I'm romanticizing labor that was never the point. The carpenter doesn't mourn the nail gun.

But I don't think it's the labor. I think it's the interval.

Between wanting something and having it, something happens to you. You carry the wanting. It changes your posture. You make decisions with it in mind. You become, incrementally, the kind of person who is *building* that thing — not the person who built it. The sixteen years weren't a failure. They were the interval. They were me slowly becoming someone with a website, someone with something to say, someone who'd earned the right to take up space online. AI collapsed the interval. The site exists. But I'm not sure I became anyone.

These words were typed, not written.

I know that sounds precious. But I mean something specific by it. Writing is slow enough that your body catches up to your thoughts. Typing is fast enough that your thoughts race ahead and your hands just chase them. What AI does is push further still — now your thoughts don't even have to fully form. You describe a direction and something else thinks it through. I sat down to write this post and I had a choice. I could have been done in thirty seconds. Instead I've been sitting here for three days, and I'm still not sure it's mine.

That's the real monkey's paw. Not that your wish comes true with a dark twist — it's that the wishing and the having collapse into each other so fast that you skip the part where you were the one who *wanted* it. You stop being a person who wants things. You become a person who specifies things.

We're entering an era of unprecedented creative output and unprecedented creative hollowness, simultaneously. The flourishing is real. The malnourishment is real. Both, at once, in the same person, staring at the same screen.

I don't know how to fix it. I'm not sure it's fixable. I'm not even sure it should be.

But when I launched this site — this thing I've wanted for sixteen years — I closed the laptop and felt the specific emptiness of a wish that granted itself.
